Lecture 1#

  • HW Problems due before class on Jan 23

  • The motion of a particle is determined by a set of coordinates. Normally, we have 3 position coordinates and 3 momentum coordinates that evolve over time: $x_{i}(t), p_{i}(t)$

  • Update Rule without any forces (apply to each particle)

    • $x_{i}(t+\Delta t) = x_{i}(t)+\frac{p_{i}(t)}{m}\delta t$
    • $p_{i}(t+\Delta t) = p_{i}(t) $

  • Update Rule without any a spring force (apply to each particle)

    • $x_{i}(t+\Delta t) = x_{i}(t)+\frac{p_{i}(t)}{m}\delta t$
    • $p_{i}(t+\Delta t) = p_{i}(t) - k(x(t)-x_{0})$

  • Another way of thinking about this is that the particle traces a path in phase space (the 6N dimensional space with 3 axes being position, and 3 axes being momentum for each particle)

    • In general, you end up with 6N coupled differential equations. Most problems don’t require all that machinery

  • Newton’s Law:

    • $\frac{dx_{i}}{dt} = \frac{p_{i}}{m}$
    • $\frac{dp_{i}}{dt} = F_{i}(t)$

  • Lagrangian Mechanics:

    • $\mathcal{L} = T(q_{i},\dot{q_{i}})-V(q_{i},\dot{q_{i}})$ where q are generalized coordinates

  • Hamiltonian Mechanics:

    • $H = T(p_{i},q_{i})+V(p_{i},q_{i})$
    • $\dot{q_{i}} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_{i}}$
    • $\dot{p_{i}} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial q_{i}}$

Lecture 2#

  • Gravitational mass (mass in Newton’s Law of Gravitation) is equivalent to inertial mass (mass in $p = mv$)
    • Equal within 1 part in $10^{-19}$
  • Cartesian Trajectory: $r_{i}(t) = \{x(t),y(t),z(t)\}$
    • $v_{i}(t) = \{\dot{x}(t),\dot{y}(t),\dot{z}(t)\}$
  • Polar Coordinates: $r_{i}(t) = r(t)\hat{r}(t)$
    • $v_{i}(t) = \dot{r}\hat{r}+r\dot{\hat{r}} = \dot{r}\hat{r}+r\dot{\theta}\hat{\theta}$
      • $\dot{\hat{\theta}} = -\dot{\theta}\hat{r}$
      • $\dot{\hat{r}} = \dot{\theta}\hat{\theta}$
    • $a_{i}(t) = (\ddot{r}-\dot{r}\dot{\theta})\hat{r}+(2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}+r\ddot{\theta})\hat{\theta}$

Lecture 3:#

  • Do the first 8 questions from the Solved Problems
  • Ex:
    • What is the force at point A that the track exerts on the object?
      • look at $a_{i}(t) = (\ddot{r}-\dot{r}\dot{\theta})\hat{r}+(2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}+r\ddot{\theta})\hat{\theta}$. $\dot{r}$ and $\ddot{r}$ is 0 because the mass is constrained to the track
      • In force from the track is confined in the radial direction, so we only look at the $\hat{r}$ direction
      • The centripetal acceleration $m\frac{v^{2}}{R}$ is provided by the gravitational force and the track force. Gravity and the track force oppose each other
      • Use conservation of energy to rewrite v in terms of h and solve for track force
    • What is the track force at B?
      • Redo the calculation for A, but instead take the height of the block to be $h-R\cos(45\degree)$
    • What is the motion of the particle after the ball leaves the track?
      • Break the motion into x and y components. Take your origin at point O. You know that the launch angle is $45\deg$. Model the y direction as a parabola and the x direction as a line. Solve fot t and plug back in for x to find the distance.
  • Say that we have some potential function $U(x)$
    • Imagine we expand the potential around a local minima located at $x_{0}$
      • $U(x) = U(x_{0})+U^{'}(x_{0})(x-x_{0})+\frac{1}{2}U^{''}(x_{0})(x-x_{0})^{2}$
        • We can redefine the potential to eliminate $U(x_{0})$ and $U^{'}(x_{0})$ equals 0. You then take the negative gradient and rearrange to get the harmonic oscillator equation around the local minima (take $x_{0}=0$):
          • $\frac{d^{2}x}{dt^{2}}+\frac{U^{''}(x_{0})}{m}x=0$s
    • We can modify the above to include damping and a driving term:
      • $\frac{d^{2}x}{dt^{2}}+\gamma\frac{dx}{dt}+\omega_{0}^{2}x=F_{drive}$

Lecture 4#

  • The trajectory in phase space of an nonlinear oscillator. The circles in the center are normal harmonic oscillations and you transition to other regimes at different positions in phase space
    • The solution when $F_{drive}=0$ is $x(t) = Re(\bar{X} exp(-i\omega t))$ with $\omega = \frac{-i\gamma}{2}\pm \sqrt{\omega_{0}^{2}-\frac{\gamma}{4}}$
      • under damped: $\omega$ has real and imaginary parts
      • critically damped: Omega only has one solution
        • In phase-space, $\frac{\dot{x}}{x} = \frac{\gamma}{2}$ defines a particular angle that all solutions approach the origin

Quality Factor#

  • Define $Q= \frac{2\omega_{0}}{\gamma} = \frac{2\pi * Energy}{Energy\ loss\ per\ cycle}$

Example 1#

  • We have an undamped linear oscillator of natural frequency $\omega_{0}$ and a step function of $a=1$ that lasts for $t=\frac{2\pi}{\omega_{0}}$. What is the response?
    • Break the problem into two initial condition problems: when t=0 and and $t = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_{0}}$. The velocity and position are zero at $t = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_{0}}$ just like $t=0$, so there is no oscillation.

Example 2#

  • An automobile with a mass of 1000 kg, including passengers settles 1.0 cm closer to the road for every additional 100 kg of passengers. It is driven with a constant horizontal component of speed 20 km/hr over a washboard road with sinusoidal bumps. The amplitude and wavelength of the sine curve are 5.0 cm and 20 cm respectively. The distance between the from and back wheels is 2.4 m. Find the amplitude of the oscillation of the automobile, assuming it moves vertically as an undamped driven harmonic oscillator. Neglect the mass of the wheels and springs and assume that the wheels are always in contact with the road.
    • We let the car be a mass of m. We attach some spring of constant k that represents the suspension system. The road provides a driving term of the form $\Epsilon(t) = \epsilon\cos(\omega t)$.
    • The equation of motion is then $m\frac{dx^{2}}{dt^{2}} = -k(X-E(t))$
      • Turn the crank to solve what the ratio of the amplitudes are

Lecture 6#

Chaos in Pendulum#

  • You have a damped pendulum driven by a sinusoidal oscillation
  • Slap in into Mathematica and examine various cases

Weakly Driven#

  • Starts with positive velocity and position of 0, then spirals to the center

Strongly Driven#

  • Creates spirals in various potential wells
  • At a stronger driving term, the spread of potential wells increases

Poincare Sections#

  • You only record the position of your pendulum in phase space every period $T$
  • Chaos is when two initial conditions very close in phase space produce vastly different results
  • In Poincare sections, chaos appears like dust scattered across the phase space
